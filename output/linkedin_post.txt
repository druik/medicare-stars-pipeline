The actual rates were 77.05% (STARS) vs 77.10% (HEDIS). The reason they appear identical turned out to be more instructive than a real divergence would have been. STARS applies frailty exclusions that HEDIS doesn't. Every excluded patient in this population had controlled BP, so removing them decreased both numerator and denominator simultaneously. At scale, that effect can move a plan across a star rating cut point on a Triple Weighted measure. On C01, the gap between 4 and 5 stars is less than 2 percentage points.

The second finding was more operational. C14 (medication adherence, also Triple Weighted) had a mean PDC of 0.835 but a plan-level rate of 70.3%, below the 2-star cut point of 72%. Ninety patients with PDC under 50% pulled the rate down despite strong average adherence. This is the math behind why MA quality programs target the lowest adherers first, not average improvement.

Two structural differences worth naming explicitly:
✔️ STARS uses fixed CMS cut points published before the measurement year. Plans can model against a known target.
✔️ HEDIS benchmarks are percentile-based and retrospective. You don't know the exact threshold until after submission closes.

Same clinical logic → Different planning horizon and financial consequences.

This work feels more relevant right now than it might have a few years ago. Star Ratings are declining across the industry, the bonus payment structure that funded a decade of supplemental benefits is under pressure, and CMS is actively overhauling the measure set. Understanding the mechanics at this level feels like the right foundation for the conversations happening in MA quality right now.
